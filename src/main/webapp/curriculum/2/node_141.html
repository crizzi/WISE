OBSERVING PROBABILITY

If we know the parents' genotypes, we can predict the probabilities of their children's genotypes.

But how can we test our predictions?

In simple cases, we can do this through hands-on experimentation.

For example, to predict the probability that a coin will land on heads when flipped, we can flip a real coin and observe what happens.

Let's say we flip the coin once and it lands on heads. Does that mean the coin will always land on heads?

Of course not! The next time you flip the coin, it may land on heads, or it may land on tails.

If we flip the coin hundreds of times, we eventually notice a PATTERN:

Half the time, the coin lands on heads, and half the time, it lands on tails.

It then becomes clear that the PROBABILITY of the coin landing on heads is about 1 in 2.

 

But how can we see similar patterns in genetic inheritance? Remember, most human parents have only 1-3 children, not hundreds!
